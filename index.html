Chapter Overview

Chapter 5 uses Zara to show how information systems and supply chain choices can create a strong competitive advantage. Zara’s model leads to competitively priced merchandise, higher margins, fewer markdowns and write-offs, faster inventory turnover, reduced risk, and less advertising pressure.

Zara vs. Gap Zara designs, produces, and distributes with much tighter coordination, while Gap relies more on seasonal design cycles and outsourced large-batch production.
5-Component IS Model Zara’s hardware, software, data, processes, and people all support the same fast-response business model.
Supply Chain Design Vertical integration, just-in-time thinking, and finely tuned logistics help Zara move concepts from idea to stores in about fifteen days on average.
SCM Risks and Tradeoffs The chapter also covers distributors, disintermediation, the bullwhip effect, outsourcing, contract manufacturing, and ethics problems in apparel supply chains.

Vocabulary and Key Concepts

These are the Chapter 5 ideas most likely to show up in scenario-based questions.

Supply Chain Management (SCM) The structuring and coordination of relationships and activities across firms to deliver value in an information- and technology-intensive global environment.
Supply Chain A network that moves inputs and finished goods from suppliers to manufacturers, distributors, retailers, and consumers.
Vertical Integration When a single firm owns several layers in its supply chain. Zara uses this to support faster coordination and data sharing.
Just-in-Time Manufacturing A system that aims to reduce excess inventory by producing and moving goods quickly and efficiently as needed.
Disintermediation Cutting out the middleman so a manufacturer ships directly to the consumer or removes one or more channel layers.
Distributor An intermediary that helps connect multiple manufacturers to multiple retailers or buyers, reducing the complexity of direct connections.
Bullwhip Effect The pattern in which variability in order size and timing increases at each stage up the supply chain, reducing overall profitability.
Interorganizational Information System A system that shares data across firms so participants can coordinate better, especially by giving everyone access to consumer-demand information.
Core Competencies The activities an organization does best and believes provide competitive advantage.
Contract Manufacturing Outsourcing production to third-party firms that make requested goods even though the brand does not own the plants or directly employ the workers.
Service Level Agreement (SLA) An agreement that defines responsibilities and expected service performance when work is handled by a third party.
RFID Radio-frequency identification tags that help track inventory quickly and accurately. Zara uses them to count store inventory more often with less labor.
Quick exam shortcut: Zara does not win by using the fanciest technology. It wins because relatively simple technology is tightly aligned with its business model, data flow, logistics, and fast decision making.

Why These Ideas Matter

This chapter matters because it shows that information systems only create value when they support what the firm is trying to do strategically. Zara spends less than the fast fashion industry average on technology, yet it uses that technology in a way that strengthens speed, coordination, and inventory control.

It also shows that supply chain choices always involve tradeoffs. Firms can outsource to lower cost and reduce capital requirements, but they may lose control, face ethical risks, or create coordination problems. Meanwhile, better information sharing can reduce the bullwhip effect, improve delivery scheduling, and help firms hold less inventory.

Chapter 5 Practice Quiz

A clothing retailer studies Zara and notices that its factories, distribution, inventory tracking, and store feedback systems all work together to get new styles into stores quickly. The retailer also notices that Zara does not simply buy a lot of flashy technology, but instead chooses tools that support its fast-response model.
1. Which statement BEST explains why Zara’s information systems create value?
  • A. Zara spends more on technology than competitors
  • B. Zara’s technology is aligned with its business model
  • C. Zara avoids using data in its supply chain
  • D. Zara focuses only on advertising systems
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The key idea is alignment between tech and strategy. Zara doesn’t overspend—it uses tech to support speed and coordination.
A rival apparel company produces large batches months in advance, while Zara can design and deliver products in about two weeks, allowing it to respond quickly to trends.
2. Which concept BEST explains Zara’s advantage?
  • A. Network effects
  • B. Vertical integration and fast logistics
  • C. Switching costs
  • D. Bundling
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Zara controls more of its supply chain and coordinates logistics tightly, enabling speed.
A manufacturer begins selling directly to customers online instead of using retailers or distributors.
3. Which concept BEST describes this decision?
  • A. Bullwhip effect
  • B. Disintermediation
  • C. Contract manufacturing
  • D. Backward compatibility
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Disintermediation means removing middlemen from the supply chain.
A company notices that small changes in customer demand cause large fluctuations in orders further up the supply chain.
4. Which concept BEST explains this?
  • A. Economies of scale
  • B. Bullwhip effect
  • C. Network effects
  • D. Convergence
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The bullwhip effect causes demand variability to increase as it moves up the chain.
A brand outsources production to third-party factories to reduce costs but later faces backlash due to poor working conditions in those factories.
5. Which concept BEST fits this situation?
  • A. Vertical integration
  • B. Contract manufacturing risks
  • C. Bundling strategy
  • D. Platform strategy
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Contract manufacturing lowers costs but introduces ethical and reputational risks.
